Q: What I need to build robot like this: https://github.com/bieli/RoboTICK/blob/master/vehicle_construction/prototypes/v1/frame__2_4/view_1.png

A: I took a look at the RoboTICK project you linked — it’s an open-source ROS-based mobile robot platform with CAD parts, electronics, and software under GPL-3.0. While the specific PNG you pointed to doesn’t render here, the repository includes vehicle construction files and a bill of materials structure under vehicle_construction.

To build a robot like that (a simple wheeled ROS-enabled mobile platform) you essentially need these categories of components:

🛠 1) Mechanical Structure (Frame & Chassis)

This is the physical skeleton of your robot.

You need:

Frame parts (aluminum profiles, 3D-printed pieces, plates)

Wheels and hubs

Mounting hardware (nuts, bolts, standoffs)

Bearings/shafts as needed

Some builders start with a ready robot chassis kit — e.g., 4WD smart car chassis — and mount their own electronics and sensors on it.

⚡ 2) Drive System

To make your robot move:

Drive Components

Motors — DC gear motors or brushless motors

Motor controllers / ESCs — H-bridge drivers, PWM controllers

Wheels or tracks

Encoders for odometry (rotation tracking)

These connect to your controller for speed and direction control.

🧠 3) Control Electronics

This is the “brain” of the robot.

Typical builds use:

A main computer like a single-board computer (SBC — e.g., Raspberry Pi, Jetson) running ROS or ROS2

A microcontroller (e.g., Arduino, ESP32) to interface with sensors and low-level control

Most mobile ROS robots separate high-level planning (on an SBC) from low-level motor control (on a microcontroller).

🔋 4) Power System

To power the robot:

A suitable rechargeable battery pack (Li-Po, Li-ion, LiFePO₄) chosen for your voltage/current needs

Power regulators to step down to 5 V/3.3 V for electronics

Connectors and a battery monitoring circuit

Proper power design is critical — robotics hardware fails if voltage collapses under load.

👀 5) Sensors & Perception

Depending on ROS capabilities you want:

IMU — inertial measurement unit for orientation

LIDAR / sonar / IR — for obstacle detection and mapping

Cameras — RGB or depth cameras for vision

Encoders — for precise wheel motion feedback

RoboTICK repos often include sensors and cameras directories for reference.

🧩 6) Software Stack

This includes:

ROS / ROS2 for communication between modules

Navigation packages (SLAM, odometry, path planning)

Drivers for sensors and motor modules

RoboTICK structure has a complete software folder covering this.

🛠️ 7) Tools

You’ll need standard assembly tools:

Screwdrivers, hex keys

Wire cutters & strippers

Soldering iron (for power and sensor wiring)

🧪 Typical Build Flow

Assemble the Frame: Cut/print parts, bolt together chassis.

Install Motors & Wheels: Attach motors with proper mounts.

Mount Electronics: Fix SBC, microcontroller, sensors to the frame.

Wire Power & Signals: Connect power distribution and signal lines.

Install Controllers: Flash microcontroller with firmware, set up motor drivers.

Software Setup: Install ROS on your SBC, integrate drivers and navigation.

🧠 Practical Tips for a First Build

Start with a ready chassis kit or a basic 4WD platform.

Begin with simple sensors (ultrasonic or IR) and progress to LIDAR.

Separate high-level logic (ROS navigation) from real-time motor control.

Make power supply robust — robotics problems often stem from undervoltage.
